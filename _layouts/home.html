Availability, Affordability, and Sustainability

of Housing, Transportation, and Utilities in South Lake Tahoe

Housing

Anybody renting a home lives in fear of losing it and not being able to find another. Kids grow up and move out and don't want to leave the community. Over 5000 people commute into work from outside of the basin. Anyobdy who wants to live in South Lake Tahoe should be able to find affordable, well-maintained housing.

We have more houses than people in Tahoe. But a lot of them aren't being used as homes. On Council, I will expand expand incentives to homeowners to rent vacant units and turn them into long term housing.

We can also build more of the housing that enables people to live here sustainably and affordably. We can follow in the tracks of Sugar Pine Village to leverage state funding for hundreds of units of deed-restricted affordable housing specifically for income-qualifying tenants, walking distance to transit, jobs, and ammenities. And we can cut the red tape to make this easier.

80% of the homes in South Lake Tahoe were built before 1980. 1% were built in the past 10 years. This moratorium on development has benefited lake clarity, but new technology and building standards present an environmental imperative to update our old buildings for energy efficiency.

Transportation

Everybody has had enough of traffic and potholes and walking in the gutter in the side of the street. But what do we expect when 96% of trips into the basin come by car? By providing other options, we can reduce traffic, road wear, emissions, and collisions. As a board member of the Lake Tahoe Bicycle Coalition, I have long advocated for active transportation infrastructure, and have been so impressed with the City's progress in recent years. I will extend the Greenway path through Bijou to Ski Run, and work with public works on low-cost connectivity enhancements in every neighborhood.

In 2020 the voters passed Measure S, increasing sales tax and quadrupling the funding for road repair to $3 million per year. I am disappointed by a regressive tax structure, where folks with the lowest income end up spending the highest fraction of it on tax, but already we have seen this new funding smooth out Pioneer trail. I'll keep up the pace of road repair, taking the opportunity to dig once for complete streets. Underground electricity, broadband, and stormwater improvements can happen at the same time, and the utilities can split the cost. Repaving Tahoe Keys Blvd will be a first priority, along with adding sidewalks and bike lanes to get people out of the gutter on this frequently used route. I'll keep a keen eye out for other budget items that we can cut to reduce the tax rate again.

I'll prioritize the transit improvements in TRPA's Regional Transportation Plan: Microtransit through neighborhoods, more frequent and night service along highway 50, and a new route up the west shore. I'll encourage Vail to run ski busses again and stop worrying about parking.

Utilities

The City has ambitious emissions targets as we do our part to Protect Our Winters and build resiliance for a changing climate. We have committed to 100% renewable electricity by 2030. Our Climate Action Plan calls for a 50% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions in the same timeframe. With 7 years to go, our next City Council will be responsible for the implementation that gets us on track. We need major investment in clean power generation and battery storage for all of our current electricity needs plus enough for our cars and home heaters.

As we shift from fossil fuels to electicity, we also need to invest in the redundancy and reliability of our grid. Undergrounding utilities will protect them from damage in the event of fires and snowstorms. Local generation and storage will provide backups against regional disruption. Government regulation will be necessary in order to keep utilities accountable to the needs of the people.
